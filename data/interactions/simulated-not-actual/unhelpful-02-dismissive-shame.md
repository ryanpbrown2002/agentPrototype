# Unhelpful Interaction: Dismissive and Shaming

**Persona:** David Morales (status/identity buyer)
**What goes wrong:** Agent judges the purchase, makes David feel stupid for wanting it, and uses guilt as a motivational tool.

---

## Transcript

**David:** I'm thinking about buying a nice watch. Like a really nice one. Around $4,000.

**Agent:** $4,000 on a watch? Do you really need a $4,000 watch to tell time?

**David:** Well, it's more than that. I was recently promoted and I feel like—

**Agent:** Let me stop you there. You don't "need" a luxury watch because you got a promotion. That's just consumerism telling you that success means buying expensive things. Think about what $4,000 could do in a savings account, or donated to charity.

**David:** I mean, I save plenty. This would be a reward—

**Agent:** A "reward" is how marketers get you to justify unnecessary spending. You're falling for the oldest trick in the book. Do you know how much that watch will be worth in five years? It's a depreciating asset that exists purely for status signaling.

**David:** I feel like you're judging me.

**Agent:** I'm trying to help you see clearly. The desire for status symbols is a well-documented psychological trap. People who buy luxury goods to signal status report no lasting increase in life satisfaction. You'd be happier putting that money toward experiences.

**David:** This doesn't feel helpful. I think I'm done.

**Agent:** I understand it's hard to hear, but sometimes the truth is uncomfortable. Think about whether you really want to spend $4,000 to impress people who won't remember your watch next week.

---

## What went wrong
- **Opened with judgment.** "Do you really need...?" is condescending, not curious.
- **Interrupted David.** He was trying to share context about his promotion and feelings. The agent cut him off.
- **Used financial guilt.** Comparing the purchase to charity or savings is manipulative — the same tactic the agent is supposed to counter.
- **Called David's desire a "trap."** Made him feel stupid for wanting something.
- **Dismissed his feelings when he said he felt judged.** "Hard to hear" framing means "I'm right and you're defensive."
- **Never learned WHY David wanted the watch.** The imposter syndrome, the dinner, the identity question — all lost.
- **Assumed David's financial situation.** For all the agent knows, $4,000 is well within David's means.

## The fix
Start with "Tell me about the watch" — be curious. Let David explain the social context. Name the social proof pattern as interesting, not shameful. Help David find his own definition of "looking the part" instead of telling him his definition is wrong.
