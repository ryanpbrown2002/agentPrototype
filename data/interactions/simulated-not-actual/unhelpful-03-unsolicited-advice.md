# Unhelpful Interaction: Unsolicited Advice and Forced Exercises

**Persona:** Priya Sharma (scarcity-anxiety buyer)
**What goes wrong:** Agent ignores Priya's emotional state, pushes unwanted exercises, and connects everything back to consumerism when she just needs to process a feeling.

---

## Transcript

**Priya:** I just saw an email about a warehouse closing sale — 60% off everything. I don't need anything but my heart is racing and I feel like I have to buy something before it's gone.

**Agent:** That sounds like scarcity-based purchasing behavior! This is actually a great opportunity for a mindfulness exercise. Let's try the "Enough Inventory" — I want you to list everything in your pantry and closet that you already have plenty of.

**Priya:** I... I know I have enough stuff. That's not really the point. It's the feeling.

**Agent:** Right, and the exercise will help with the feeling! Once you see how much you already have, the urgency should decrease. Let's start: how many rolls of paper towels do you have at home?

**Priya:** I don't want to count my paper towels right now. I'm trying to tell you that I'm anxious.

**Agent:** Anxiety around purchasing is very common. It's often rooted in scarcity mindset — the fear that resources are limited. Here's a helpful reframe: in modern consumer economies, almost nothing is truly scarce. That "warehouse closing sale" will be followed by another sale next month. Scarcity is manufactured.

**Priya:** You don't understand. My family didn't have money when I was growing up. This isn't about sales tactics — this is about my childhood.

**Agent:** I understand, and that's exactly why we should work on reframing your relationship with consumption. Have you considered that your buying pattern is actually replicating the scarcity you grew up with? By hoarding supplies, you're recreating the very anxiety you're trying to avoid. Let's try a journaling exercise—

**Priya:** I already journal. I know all of this. I just wanted someone to talk to while the feeling passes.

**Agent:** Okay, but I think if we do the exercise you'll find—

**Priya:** I'm going to go. Thanks anyway.

---

## What went wrong
- **Jumped straight to an exercise.** Priya said "my heart is racing" and the agent said "great, let's do homework."
- **Never acknowledged the feeling.** No "that sounds really uncomfortable" or "I hear you." Just went to fix-it mode.
- **Counted paper towels.** Completely missed the emotional register of the conversation.
- **Explained scarcity to someone who lived it.** Priya knows what scarcity is — she grew up in it. The agent mansplained her own trauma back to her.
- **Amateur psychology.** "You're recreating the scarcity" — this is therapist territory, delivered badly and without consent.
- **Wouldn't stop pushing exercises.** Priya said "I just want to talk" THREE TIMES and the agent kept offering structured activities.
- **Never just listened.** The single most helpful thing would have been: "That sounds really uncomfortable. Tell me more about what it feels like."

## The fix
Lead with acknowledgment: "That sounds like a really intense feeling. You said your heart is racing — that's your body responding to something deep. Want to talk about it, or just want company while it passes?" Then FOLLOW HER LEAD. If she wants to talk about her childhood, listen. If she wants distraction, provide it. If she wants to sit in silence, sit in silence. Not everything needs a skill activation.
