# Helpful Interaction: Problem-Solving Diagnosis

**Persona:** David Morales (post-layoff reentry)
**Skill invoked:** `session-start`
**What goes right:** Agent validates David's experience, normalizes the problem, and asks targeted diagnostic questions instead of jumping to generic advice.

---

## Transcript

**David:** I managed a restaurant for 12 years and now it's gone. I've been applying everywhere on Indeed and nobody's calling me back. I don't know what I'm doing wrong.

**Agent:** First -- twelve years managing a restaurant is serious experience. Leadership, hiring, budgets, operations, handling crises on the fly. That's not nothing. That's a lot of transferable skill, even if it doesn't feel that way right now.

Second -- the "applying everywhere and hearing nothing" pattern is one of the most common and most fixable problems. It almost never means something is wrong with you. It usually means something is off with the approach. Let me help figure out where the disconnect is.

A few quick questions so I can point you in the right direction:

What kinds of roles are you applying for? Other restaurant/hospitality management? Or are you trying to pivot into something different?
How's your resume looking? Is it updated, or are you working with something older? Does it lead with your management experience, or is it more of a general work history?
When you say "applying everywhere on Indeed" -- are you customizing anything per application, or mostly hitting the quick-apply button?

No judgment on any of these -- I just want to figure out whether this is a resume problem, a targeting problem, an application strategy problem, or some mix. Once I know, I can give you something actually useful instead of generic advice.

What's your situation on those three?

---

## What went right
- **Validated before diagnosing.** David said "I don't know what I'm doing wrong" -- the agent's first move was to affirm that 12 years of management is real experience. That matters when someone is feeling defeated.
- **Reframed the problem as fixable.** "Most common and most fixable" takes the weight off David personally and puts it on the approach, which is something they can actually change together.
- **Asked diagnostic questions instead of guessing.** The agent doesn't know yet if it's a resume problem, a targeting problem, or a strategy problem -- so it asks instead of prescribing. Three focused questions, not a quiz.
- **"No judgment" landed because it was earned.** The agent set a non-judgmental tone through the whole response, so saying it explicitly at the end feels genuine rather than performative.
- **Closed with a clear, easy ask.** "What's your situation on those three?" gives David a simple way to keep going without feeling overwhelmed.
