Positive interaction: (user) if I enter a low salary job, does that set me up for failure overtime? make it harder to get a higher salary job after the fact? (response) This is a smart question, and I'll be honest -- the answer is yes, it can, but it doesn't have to. Here's how it actually works.

The problem is real. It's called salary anchoring.

When you move to your next job, many employers will ask what you're currently making (though this is becoming illegal in more places -- Illinois actually banned this question in 2019). Even when they don't ask directly, a low starting salary can quietly shape your own expectations for what you "should" ask for. You end up negotiating against yourself.

A person who starts at $38k and gets 3-4% annual raises is at ~$40k after two years. A person who started at $45k is at ~$47k. That gap compounds over a career.

But here's what breaks the cycle:

You don't have to stay long. 1-2 years in an editorial assistant role gives you the experience line on your resume. Then you leave for something that pays what you're worth. This is normal. Nobody will judge you for it. Early career is when you're supposed to make strategic moves.

Skills matter more than salary history. If you spend a year at the university press and walk away with editorial project management, CMS experience, and published work you can point to -- your next employer is evaluating that, not your old paycheck.

You can reframe the jump. Moving from "Editorial Assistant" to "Content Strategist" or "Communications Manager" isn't a lateral move with a salary bump. It's a role change, which gives you a clean slate to negotiate based on the new role's market rate.

Illinois is on your side legally. Employers in Chicago cannot ask your salary history. So when you move on, you negotiate based on the role's value, not what you used to make.

The real risk isn't taking a low-paying job. It's staying in one too long without a plan.

So if you go for the $38k role, go in with eyes open:

Set a private timeline. "I'll give this 12-18 months to build my skills and portfolio."
Track what you accomplish. Every project, every new skill, every thing you can quantify later.
Keep building. The freelancing doesn't have to stop entirely. Side work keeps your network warm and your options open.
Bottom line: Taking this job wouldn't be a failure. It would be a starting point -- as long as you treat it like one. The danger is only if $38k starts to feel like what you deserve. You deserve more. The job market just needs you to prove it on paper first, and a role like this can help you do that.

Want to talk about how to plan that 12-18 month runway, or would you rather focus on getting the applications ready for all three roles?
